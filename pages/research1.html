Introduction

  • Millets, among the earliest foods known to humanity, have been discovered in charred grain form at Harappan sites and are mentioned in the Vedas, ancient archaeological and literary sources of the Indian subcontinent. Scientifically categorized as small-seeded cereal grains within the grass family (Poaceae), millets exhibit diverse species, colors, and appearances, cultivated in numerous countries worldwide.
  • They fall into two main categories based on size: major millets (e.g., sorghum, pearl millet, finger millet) and minor millets (e.g., little millet, foxtail millet, proso millet, barnyard millet, kodo millet).
  • These ancient grains have served various purposes, being consumed by humans and utilized as animal feed throughout history. Millets are important staple food, particularly, in poor, semi-arid tropics of Asia and Africa (Mahendra 2012 and Narloch et al. , 2009) which are mostly cultivated under a variety of agro-ecological situations like plains, coast hills even diverse soil land varying rainfall.
  • They are most popular in developing regions, like India and Africa, where food and nutritional security are the major challenges. Millets are one of the most drought resistant crops and have immense production potential with minimal inputs.
  • According to FAOSTAT data from 2023, global millet production during 2019-2020 was 84.17 million metric tons, grown across 70.75 million hectares of land. India contributed 20.50 percent of this production.

Malting

  • Processing of millets improves its nutritional availability and digestibility. Malting is such biological process which improves the overall acceptability of millets. Malting leads to the production of various hydrolytic enzymes and bioactive compounds. It is a controlled technique involving the partial germination of cereal grains (Banusha & Vasantharuba, 2013). During malting, grains are hydrated under controlled conditions, triggering the activation of endogenous enzymes that transform the grain's constituents, such as soluble sugars, proteins, and enzyme activity (Nadeem et al. , 2010).
  • This process enhances the nutritional content and digestibility of grains, making it a simple method to increase iron and other mineral availability from grains. Local crops like finger millet and moong bean are commonly used for malting (Platel et al. , 2010). Research has shown that germination significantly increases the protein content and nutritional profile of grains.

Importance of Malting

  • Increase in availability of vitamins and minerals. Increase in nutrient density. Decrease in levels of antinutritional factors like phytate and tannins. Not only is malt itself a nutrient-rich food, it can be added to unmalted grain foods to improve their nutrient density and palatability.
    1. Nutritional Importance
      • Enhanced Nutritional Value: Malting improves the nutritional profile of grains by increasing the availability of vitamins (especially B vitamins), minerals (like iron and zinc), and amino acids.
      • Reduction of Anti-Nutrients: Anti-nutrients such as phytic acid and tannins, which inhibit the absorption of essential minerals, are reduced during malting, thus enhancing the bioavailability of nutrients.
      • Improved Mineral Availability: The malting process breaks down complex carbohydrates and proteins into simpler, more digestible forms, making the grains easier to digest and the nutrients more accessible.
    2. Functional Importance
      • Flavor and Texture Enhancement: Malting develops natural flavors and improves the texture of grains, making them more appealing for consumption. This is especially beneficial in food products where taste and mouthfeel are critical.
      • Enzyme Activation: The malting process activates enzymes such as amylases, which convert starches into fermentable sugars. This is essential for brewing and baking industries where fermentation is required.
      • Food Product Versatility: Malted grains can be used to create a variety of products, including malted flours, beverages, breakfast cereals, and health foods.
    3. Economic Importance
      • Value Addition: Malting transforms raw grains into higher-value products, providing better returns for farmers and producers.
      • Market Expansion: The process enables the development of new products and markets, expanding the commercial potential of grains beyond traditional uses.
      • Increased Shelf Life: Malting can enhance the shelf life of grain-based products by reducing moisture content and inhibiting microbial growth, thus reducing food waste.
    4. Health Benifits
      • Functional Foods: Malted grains can be used to develop functional foods and beverages that offer health benefits, such as probiotic drinks and fortified foods.
      • Dietary Improvement: By improving the nutritional content and digestibility of grains, malting supports better dietary outcomes, especially in regions where nutrient deficiencies are prevalent.
    5. Environmental and Sustainability Aspects
      • Promotion of Climate-Resilient Crops: Malting encourages the cultivation of grains like millets, which are resilient to climate change and require fewer resources, contributing to sustainable agriculture.
      • Efficient Resource Use: The process makes better use of agricultural resources by converting grains into a variety of valuable products, thereby reducing waste and improving resource efficiency.

Millets Scenario - India and the World

  • India is the largest producer of millets in the world. In India, Millets are grown in about 21 States. There is a major impetus in Rajasthan, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Telangana, Uttarakhand, Jharkhand, Madhya Pradesh, Haryana and Gujarat.

  • In India, millets are cultivated in an area of 13.83 million hectares, producing 15.53 million tonnes with a yield of 1248 kg/ha. Sorghum is the fourth most important food grain in India after rice, wheat, and maize in terms of area (4.83 Mn. ha) and production (4.31 Mn. MT). Bajra (7.55 m ha) is contributing more than 50 per cent of the country's area under millets with nearly equal percentage of production. It is interesting to note that, India is the topmost producer of Barnyard (99.9%), Finger (53.3%), Kodo (100%), Little millet (100%) and pearl millet (44.5%), producing about 12.46 million metric tonnes from an area of 8.87 million ha.
  • Rajasthan has the highest area under millets cultivation (29.05%) followed by Maharashtra (20.67%), Karnataka (13.46%), Uttar Pradesh (8.06%), Madhya Pradesh (6.11%), Gujarat (3.94%) and Tamil Nadu (3.74%).
  • According to FAO, the world production of millets is 89.17 million metric tonnes from an area of 74.00 million hectare during 2020. Sorghum and Pearl millet are the major millet crops grown, constituting above 90 percent of the world millets production followed by Finger millet, Foxtail millet, Proso millet, Barnyard, Little millet and Kodo millet.
Nutritional Composition
  • Sudha (2012) examined the proximate composition of finger millet (Eleusine coracana L.), highlighting its nutritional significance. Finger millet is valued in India as a staple food for economically disadvantaged groups. It is noted for its high levels of calcium (0.38%), dietary fiber (18%), and phenolic compounds (0.3-3%). These components contribute to its recognized health benefits, including anti-diabetic, anti-tumorigenic, anti-atherosclerogenic, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties.
  • Malik (2013) concluded that pearl millet is not expensive like pearl but it's definitely has pearl like quality which is beneficial to the body. 100 grams of bajra has the following nutritional values: energy 360 calories, moisture 12g, protein 12g, fat 5g, mineral 2g, fiber 1g, carbohydrate 67g, Calcium 42mg, phosphorus 242mg, and iron 8mg.
  • Kindiki et al ., (2015) stated that malnutrition is a common occurrence in the arid and semi-arid lands (ASALs) of Kenya. Therefore nutrition efforts should be geared towards achieving affordable and nutritious complementary foods for children who are the most vulnerable group in the society. The study evaluated the effects of germination of pearl millet (Pennisetum glaucum) for up to 5 days followed by fermentation (24 hrs) and roasting on proximate composition, iron, zinc, calcium and phytates contents. Results showed that this process decreased calcium and phytic acid levels significantly (p<0.05) while protein, ash, crude fiber, iron and zinc levels were increased. The germinated, fermented and roasted flour gave porridge of superior sensory quality than flour from untreated millet.
  • Chauhan et al. (2017) reviewed that millets have strong nutritional and nutraceutical components and are equivalent to other cereals in terms of caloric, carbohydrate, and protein content, but they include higher fat, calcium, iron, dietary fibre, and vitamin E (tocopherols and tocotrienols). These have been discovered to be high in phytochemicals such phenolic acids, flavonoids, catechins, phytic acid, and phytosterols, the inclusion of dietary fibre and phenolic chemicals aids in the prevention of several illnesses, including diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and cataractogenesis. These phytochemicals have also been shown to have antioxidant and antibacterial effects.
  • Patekar and Hashmi (2017) evaluated three sorghum varieties viz. M 35-1 (Maldandi), CSV-22R, and Parbhani Jyoti for their physico-chemical properties and mineral content. The genotype M 35-1 had a significantly higher level of protein (10.49%) and the presence of lipids, dietary fibre, tannins, and minerals. The genotype M 35-1 also had a higher level of phosphorus content (515 mg/100g) and iron content (4.26 mg/100g). The highest value of calcium content (27.50 mg/100g) and magnesium content (228 mg/100g) was found in Parbhani Jyoti, while the lowest magnesium content (213 mg/100g) was found in CSV-22R.
  • Chauhan (2018) conducted a comparative study of the nutrient and anti-nutrient contents of raw and germinated finger millet. The total ash content was 2.8±0.17 for Whole Raw Finger Millet Flour (WRFMF) and 2.7±0.10 for Germinated Finger Millet Flour (GFMF). The protein content was 6.3±0.20 in WRFMF and 8.8±0.30 in GFMF. Mineral analysis revealed that calcium and iron content increased significantly during germination. The study concluded that germination enhances the nutritional profile and reduces anti-nutrients in finger millet, improving its acceptability, digestibility, and nutrient bioavailability.
  • Erasmus et al. (2018) studied the effect of processing methods on the proximate composition of finger millets. The results showed that fermented millet had higher carbohydrate content (78.46%), crude fiber (8.48%), and energy (327.96 Kcal/100g), while roasted millet had higher crude protein (6.53%) and moisture (11.25%). Bulk density of fermented millet was 1158 ± 16.51 kg/m³, significantly different from roasted and unprocessed millets, which had 993.6 ± 11.44 kg/m³ and 1146.80 ± 16.04 kg/m³, respectively. Fermented millet had a DPPH activity of 26.40% and foaming capacity of 1.96%, significantly different from roasted and unprocessed millet, both of which had 31.80% DPPH activity and 5.88% foaming capacity.
  • Iyabo et al. (2018) evaluated the effects of sprouting on the proximate and mineral composition of white sorghum bicolor and pennisetum glaucum. The sprouting process resulted in a significant increase (p<0.05) in crude protein, crude fibre, and ash content in both grains. The moisture content of sorghum bicolor increased with sprouting, while that of pennisetum glaucum decreased, implying an increased shelf life for pennisetum glaucum. The study concluded that sprouting improved the nutrient composition and enhanced the functional properties of both sorghum bicolor and pennisetum glaucum, with pennisetum glaucum being slightly better in terms of shelf life.
  • Morya et al.(2018) concluded that sorghum grain contains 8 to 12 percent protein, 65 to 76 percent starch with approximately 2 percent fibre. The germ, a rich source of oil (28% of the germ) also has high levels of protein (19%) and ash (10%) and is significantly rich in resistant starch, soluble and insoluble dietary fibers, minerals and antioxidants. It contains 2.8 percent crude fiber, 7.8 percent crude fat, 13.6 percent crude protein, and 63.2 percent starch.
  • Singh et al. (2018) determined that the pearl millet has deep root system so it extract soil nutrient and holds higher nutritional value than the other cereal crops such as wheat, rice, maize and sorghum. Mineral-wise, this crop contain high amount of iron, zinc, magnesium, copper, manganese, potassium and phosphorous. It is good source of energy, with calorific value of 361 Kcal/100g and high in fiber content (1.2g / 100g).
  • Jandu and Kawatra (2019) studied the nutritional composition of different varieties of pearl millet. The nutritional evaluation indicated that the moisture content of pearl millet varieties HC-20 (bio-fortified), HHB-67 (improved), and WHC 901 was 7.41 per cent, 8.61 per cent, and 7.89 per cent, respectively. The protein content was 11.95 g/100g in HC-20 (bio-fortified), 12.48 g/100g in HHB-67 (improved), and 11.81 g/100g in WHC-901. The crude fibre content was highest (1.67%) in the HHB-67 (improved) variety. The mineral content analysis showed that the highest iron (7.23 mg/100g) and zinc (4.35 mg/100g) contents were found in the HC-20 (bio-fortified) variety, while the highest calcium content (54.87 mg/100g) was found in the HHB-67 (improved) variety.
  • Kumari et al. (2019) developed a nutrient-rich Ready-to-Eat (RTE) breakfast cereal by incorporating standardized proportions of popped pearl millet along with other ingredients. The cereal contained high levels of proteins (9.34±0.12%), dietary fibers (6.13±0.14%), folic acid (103.21±0.04 mg/100g), and minerals such as calcium (62.79±0.06 mg/100g), phosphorus (239.53±0.88 mg/100g), iron (5.02±0.02 mg/100g), and zinc (3.24±0.03 mg/100g).
  • A study by Ogodo et al.(2019) and Mohapatra et al. (2019) indicated that sorghum is very closely related to maize in a nutritional relation. The two cereals contain almost the same protein, carbohydrates, crude fibre, fat and oils.
  • Soubhagyalaxmi and Geetha (2019) study the nutritional composition of raw, malted and popped finger millet varieties. Five varieties of finger millet namely GPU66, GPU67, GPU71, MR6 and PR202 were investigated for their nutritional contents before and after processing such as malting and popping. Protein content of raw finger millet varieties ranged from 6.25 to 11.87g/100g. Malted flour crude fibre ranged from 2.35 to 3.10g/100g and that in popped flour from 2.00 to 2.45g/100g. GPU66 and GPU67 were found to be nutritionally better compared to other varieties both in raw and processed forms.
  • According to USDA (2019), "Sorghum grain contains on an average 10.6 g proteins, 72.09 g carbohydrates, 6.7 g fibers, 3.46 g lipids and provides 1377 kJ energy per 100 g". The unusual composition of sorghum phenolic compounds (phenolic acids, 3-deoxyanthocyanins, flavones, flavanones and condensed tannins) and their high level reported in sorghum bran make it an interesting cereal for the production of healthy functional foods as a source of bioactive compounds.
  • Alane et al. (2021) investigated the chemical properties of sorghum and recorded 7.9 percent moisture, 10.62 percent protein, 1.73 percent fat, 73.85 percent carbohydrates, 1.31 percent ash and 2.77 percent crude fibre.
  • Dharmar et al.(2021) analyzed the moisture, protein, crude fat, ash, carbohydrate, crude fibre and energy value of sorghum grain were 11.08g, 10.27g, 3.26g, 1.12g, 72.91g, 1.65g and 359.19 kcal per 100g respectively. Minerals calcium, phosphorous, iron and zinc of sorghum grain were 21.40, 210.03, 4.14, and 1.65 mg/100g respectively. Likewise moisture, protein, crude fat, crude fibre, ash, carbohydrate and energy value of pearl millet was 12.20g, 11.59g, 4.4g, 1.55g, 1.6, 68.64g and 360.6 kcal per 100g respectively. Ca, P, Fe and Zn mineral content of pearl millet was 30.40, 292.08, 8.45 and 7.14mg/100g respectively. The proximate analysis revealed that these grains have appreciable amounts of nutrients.
  • Ogonna et al. (2022) concluded that sorghum flours had 4.35±0.11 percent, 2.6±0.23 percent, 0.26±0.03 percent, 3.28±0.06 percent, 2.16±0.10 percent and 25.02±1.02 percent respectively for moisture, ash content, fat content, crude protein, crude fibre and carbohydrate content.
  • Dias et al. (2024) studied the impact of three processing methods (decortication, sprouting, and extrusion) on the nutritional and technological characteristics of pearl millet flour. The extruded flour had the highest water solubility index (WSI), the lowest lipid content, and paste viscosity. The sprouted flour showed low setback, paste viscosity, and phytate content, along with high protein and dietary fiber content. The study highlighted the nutritional benefits of sprouted flour and suggested promoting millet flour production for various food applications, particularly in regions with large cultivated areas of this cereal, often used for animal feed, and an expanding gluten-free market.
Phytochemical Analysis

  • Millets are considered Nutri-cereals and are also known to contribute to potential health benefits. Phytochemicals present in millets contribute in enhancing the nutritional value of grains .According to the classification given by Indian Institute of Minor millets, they are mainly grouped as kodo millet, barnyard millet, porso millet, little millet, and foxtail millet and are reported to be mainly rich in phenols, flavonoids and phytosterols.
  • Abioye et al. (2018) evaluated the effects of germination on the phytochemical properties of Finger Millet Flour. The study found that germination significantly (p<0.05) decreased total phenolic content from 42.63 per cent to 38.46 per cent and tannin content from 61.66 per cent to 33.33 per cent. Conversely, flavonoid content and antioxidant activity increased significantly (p<0.05) from 26.66 per cent to 33.33 per cent and 48.30 per cent to 51.13 per cent, respectively. The study concluded that germination enhances the antioxidant activity of finger millet and reduces its anti-nutritional contents, increasing its potential as a functional food.
  • Annor et al. (2018) revealed that millet grains contain phytochemicals such as phenolic acids, flavonoids, and phytates, which may inhibit the action of gastrointestinal enzymes like α-amylase (pancreatic) and α-glucosidase (intestinal) that hydrolyze starch, oligosaccharides, and disaccharides into monosaccharides. This inhibition can reduce body hyperglycemia, highlighting millet's potential in managing blood sugar levels.
  • Erasmus et al. (2018) determined the effect of processing methods on the phytochemical composition of finger millets. The study found that terpenoids, tannins, steroids, and saponin were present in fermented millet, while only alkaloids, terpenoids, and tannin were present in roasted millet. The presence of these phytochemicals indicates that processing methods can influence the phytochemical profile of finger millets, with fermented millet being particularly rich in health-benefiting phytochemicals.
  • Chauhan (2018) conducted a comparative study on the phytochemical composition of raw and germinated finger millet. The tannin contents of raw finger millet (WRFMF) and germinated finger millet (GFMF) were measured at 870.8±1.05 and 360.5±0.10 mg/100g, respectively. Phytic acid content in raw finger millet was 851.4±1.6 mg/100g, whereas germinated finger millet showed a reduced content of 238.5±1.3 mg/100g (GFMF). Oxalic acid content and trypsin inhibitor activity were significantly decreased after the germination process. These findings indicate that germination enhances the nutritional profile of finger millet by reducing anti-nutrient content.
  • Xiong et al. (2019) highlighted that phenolic compounds in sorghum primarily consist of phenolic acids, 3-deoxyanthocyanidins, and condensed tannins. These compounds exhibit strong antioxidant activity in vitro, and regular consumption of sorghum whole grain is associated with potential improvements in gut health and reduction in chronic disease risks. Meanwhile, Sudha (2012) conducted a study focusing on the functional and phytochemical properties of finger millet (Eleusine coracana L.). Finger millet is recognized for its polyphenol and dietary fiber contents, making it beneficial for health. It is a staple food in India, particularly for low-income groups, and is notable for its high calcium content (0.38%), dietary fiber (18%), and phenolic compounds (0.3-3%). These compounds contribute to its health-promoting effects, including anti-diabetic, anti-tumorigenic, anti-atherosclerogenic, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties.
  • Mounika et al. (2022) conducted a study on the estimation of phytochemicals in millets and selected millet products. The research involved qualitative and quantitative analysis of phytochemicals in aqueous extracts from eight millets: Jowar, Korralu, Ragi, Bajra, Udalu, Arikalu, Varigalu, and Samalu. The findings revealed that these millets and millet products exhibited significant phytochemical and nutrient compositions. Foxtail millet showed the highest flavonoid content at 7.808 mg/g, while barnyard millet had the highest alkaloid content at 2.149 mg/g, and finger millet had the least at 0.058 mg/g. The study emphasized that these phytochemicals, including phenols, tannins, flavonoids, alkaloids, and terpenoids, while not directly involved in maintaining body health, play a crucial role in treating chronic diseases. The millet-based food products developed in the study were shown to nourish the body and potentially prevent various diseases such as diabetes, cardiovascular diseases, cancer, inflammation, gastric issues, and other disorders.

Development of Product

  • Bolarinwa et al. (2016) aimed to create quality biscuits using blends of malted-sorghum and soybean flour, alongside wheat flour as a control. They evaluated the biscuits for composition, minerals, and sensory properties. The biscuits made from these blends showed increased protein (7.3% to 11.7%), fat (27.4% to 30.8%), fiber (2.8% to 3.5%), and ash (2.2% to 2.8%), while carbohydrate content decreased (56.7% to 47.1%) with higher soy flour substitution. Mineral analysis indicated higher iron, zinc, and calcium levels, but lower phosphorus. Sensory evaluation rated all biscuits highly across tested attributes. This development enhances nutrient intake, especially for children, and promotes sorghum use in countries like Nigeria.
  • Tanwar and Dhillon (2017) developed gluten free cookies using flour of bajra, buckwheat and ragi in different combinations. They reported that cookies made with 100: 60: 40 were most acceptable.
  • Kumari et al. (2019) developed a nutrient-rich Ready-to-Eat (RTE) breakfast cereal by incorporating standardized proportions of popped pearl millet along with popped amaranth, puffed wheat, flax seeds, sunflower seeds, raisins, honey, sugar, oil, and water. The sensory evaluation indicated highly acceptable attributes, and the cereal had a shelf life of 3 minutes.
  • Momanyi et. al., (2020) formulate and analyze the physical, nutritional and sensory attributes of baobab based ready-to-eat sorghum and cowpea blend snack bars. Popped sorghum, baked cowpeas and baobab pulp powder were blended in five different formulations (45:55:0; 50:45:5; 55:35:5; 55:35:10; 60:25:15; 65:15:20) to produce ready-to-eat (RTE) snack bars. The nutrient composition, physical properties and sensory qualities of the bars were analyzed and significant difference between means determined by Tukey test, at p<0.05.
  • Chitra et al. (2020) investigated the impact of replacing wheat flour with finger millet and pearl millet flours on the quality characteristics and nutritional composition of Belgian waffles. They found that control waffles without millet flours had a harder texture (shear force of 35.86N), whereas waffles containing 30% and 40% finger millet exhibited the highest acceptability in sensory attributes. The incorporation of millet flours did not adversely affect the crumb integrity or structure of the waffles. Furthermore, substituting wheat flour with millets led to an increase in total dietary fiber, while reducing carbohydrate and caloric content. The experimental waffles also showed higher levels of slowly digestible starch (SDS) and resistant starch (RS), with the highest amounts of insoluble dietary fiber (ISDF) (6.46%) and soluble dietary fiber (SDF) (1.29%) observed in waffles containing 30% finger millet and 20% pearl millet. Additionally, all experimental waffles demonstrated a significant increase in mineral content, indicating that the use of millets can effectively enhance the nutritional profile of waffles.
  • Alane et al. , (2021) prepared instant sorghum tortilla mix with flour of malted sorghum, bengal gram, soybean and fenugreek leaves powder with different ratio i.e. 70:23:05:02, 70:17:10:03, 70:11:15:04, 70:05:20:05 respectively and malted sorghum flour and bengal gram flour were used for preparation of control sample. Prepared instant sorghum tortilla mix was analyzed for chemical and sensory properties. Sensory evaluation revealed that the sample T3 secured highest score and which contains fat 5.02 percent, protein 15.53 percent, carbohydrate 66.02 percent, crude fibre 3.46 percent and energy value 371.38 kcal respectively. The prepared instant mix can be stored in laminated aluminium pouch for 90 days without any microbial deterioration.
  • Divakar & Prakash (2021) developed khakhra, incorporating different millets namely foxtail millet, finger millet, pearl millet and sorghum as variants in wholegrain composite flour. The nutritional composition and shelf-stability was analyzed by using standard protocols. Sensory evaluation showed that finger millet incorporated product had low acceptability compared to the rest of millets used.
  • Mounika and Sireesha (2021) developed multi grain bread using flour of wheat, pearl millet, sorghum, along with yogurt, sugar, butter and yeast. The bread was processed in 3 different ratios of multi millet flour. The sensorial study showed high acceptance of variation II compared to variation I and variation III. The Nutrient composition was compared with normal bread and it showed rich nutrients like proteins, fiber, calcium and iron etc. Thus proving it to be a better product for health conscious consumers.
  • Bashir et al. (2022) investigated the nutritional composition and sensory properties of cookies made from a combination of finger millet and wheat flour. Various ratios (100% wheat flour, 100% finger millet, and blends of 95%:5%, 90%:10%, and 80%:20% wheat to finger millet) were formulated and evaluated. Proximate composition analysis showed significant differences, with the 95% wheat and 5% finger millet ratio exhibiting distinct nutritional qualities compared to other ratios and individual flours. Sensory evaluation revealed that cookies from the 95% wheat and 5% finger millet ratio were highly preferred by respondents for taste, flavor, and texture compared to cookies from other compositions. This indicates that the product developed with the 95% wheat and 5% finger millet ratio was the most acceptable. The study supports the consumption of such products as they are highly encouraged and cost-effective.
  • Curti et al. (2022) explored the use of sorghum flour as a gluten-free alternative in sponge cake formulations, addressing the increasing demand for such products. They characterized different sorghum flours produced from white and brown sorghum grains through milling and evaluated their impact on batter properties and sponge cake quality. The study focused on flour composition, particle size distribution, damaged starch content, and water absorption characteristics. Results indicated that milling processes significantly influenced flour composition and properties. Decreasing particle size led to higher pasting viscosity, denser batters, and more uniform distribution of small air bubbles in cakes made from both brown and white sorghum flour. Smaller particle size flours consistently resulted in sponge cakes with higher volume and lower firmness, irrespective of sorghum variety used.
  • Al Majzoub et al. , (2023) investigated the production of biscuits from composite flour wheat and sorghum. Biscuits produced from 30 percent sorghum flour were shown to be the best concentration in terms of quality, acceptability and sensory properties. The maximum sensory overall acceptability scores were found for biscuits prepared from substitution levels (20-40%).
  • Sai and Devi (2023) focused on developing a product to address micronutrient deficiencies often overlooked in mainstream nutritional studies that prioritize calorie intake. They developed Ragi Brownie as a solution for calcium deficiency, emphasizing sensory evaluation, nutrient analysis, and shelf-life. Three variations of Ragi Brownie were initially developed, with the version approved by the sensory panel undergoing further analysis. Finger Millet (Ragi) was chosen for its high calcium content and ecological sustainability, as well as its resilience to various climates. The development process aimed to simplify production and reduce costs, facilitating smooth adoption and achieving desired nutritional outcomes.

Website Development

  • Shaban et al. , 2011 generated a consistent, multidisciplinary semantic platform that assembles social, environmental, economical and behavioural knowledge in the domain of healthy eating and child obesity prevention. Such biomedical ontologies, like the Childhood Obesity Prevention [Knowledge] Enterprise (COPE), include advanced logical reasoning services and social networking in order to develop an integrated consensus knowledge base. COPE can offer dietary recommendations based on the individual's personal profile for promoting healthy eating habits.
  • Takahashi et al. , 2012 presented an Automatic Nutrient Calculation System on the web, which identifies and calculates the amount of the ingredients in grams included in almost 1 million registered recipes as well as the total amount of nutrients in each one of the ingredients. In the 34 ingredient nutrient database, information on the cooking processing method, change the quantity of nutrients.
  • Chena et al. , 2012 launched a web based nutritional diagnosis expert system for dietetic professionals in Taiwan. This expert system assists in the nutritional-related decision making by generating a patient's nutrition diagnosis, which is a three-faceted process. First of all, the system takes into consideration the nutritional conditions and problems associated with each patient. Second, an etiology of the patient's condition is described in order to set the goals for the nutritional care intervention. Finally, the patient's sign and symptoms are recorded, including biochemical data, anthropometric measurements, physical examination findings and nutrition history. Therefore, the nutrition diagnosis expert system offers a holistic approach in nutrition diagnosis and management, as it comprises both identification of the problem and the means to deal with it in a standardized, profound and efficient way.
  • Gibney et al. , 2013 argued that the use of web-based, personalized nutrition applications for the collection of food data in an intervention study should be, first of all, plausible and user-friendly, even though precise and exact data entry from the participants is less possible. Furthermore, personalized feedback towards the participants should be easy-to-use, based on simple visual tools, instead of any numeric data. Food choice advice should be focused on meal intake and ranges of nutrient intake, presented on the computer screen by making a classification, depending on the participant's average nutritional needs.
  • Morrison et al. , 2014 depicted that this study provides evidence that Smartphone has the potential to improve individual's engagement with their health-related goals when used as a supplement to an existing online intervention. The perceived convenience of mobile access to information does not appear to deter use of Web-based interventions or strengthen the impact of app access on goal engagement. A mixed-methods design enabled exploration of individual variation in daily usage of the app-based tools.
